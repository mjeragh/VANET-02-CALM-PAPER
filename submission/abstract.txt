Coordinating highway exits in dense traffic requires connected autonomous vehicles to execute precise, tightly coupled lane-change and speed-adjustment maneuvers. Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) offers a scalable framework for this task, but MARL policies trained for cooperative driving are plagued by mode collapse: they converge to a single repeated action (selecting "Maintain" 99.7% of the time in our environment), abandoning the lane-change and acceleration behaviors the task requires. Standard remedies---entropy regularization, exploration bonuses, and value decomposition---fail to resolve this degeneracy, as we demonstrate across an entropy-coefficient sweep and a multi-agent SAC baseline. We present CALM (Constrained Actor-Learning from deMonstrations), a hybrid imitation–reinforcement learning algorithm that prevents mode collapse through behavioral cloning (BC) pre-training on human driving data, followed by RL fine-tuning with a decaying KL-divergence constraint anchored to the BC prior. CALM is evaluated against six comparators---DAgger, GAIL, entropy-regularized MADDPG, QMIX-CALM, MASAC, and a rule-based controller---on a cooperative highway exit coordination task validated against the NGSIM naturalistic driving dataset. In a five-seed comparative study, DAgger achieves the highest NGSIM action agreement at 45.3% +/- 0.9% (p < 0.001 vs. all others), while CALM and QMIX-CALM eliminate mode collapse (action entropy 0.70-0.88 vs. 0.06 for the entropy-only baseline) and attain the highest RL reward among methods that preserve diversity (1.35 +/- 0.05). Anchoring MARL policies to human demonstrations via decaying KL regularization is an effective strategy for producing diverse, human-like cooperative driving behaviors.
