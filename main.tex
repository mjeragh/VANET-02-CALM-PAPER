\begin{abstract}
Coordinating highway exits in dense traffic requires connected autonomous vehicles to execute precise, tightly coupled lane-change and speed-adjustment maneuvers. Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) offers a scalable framework for this task, but MARL policies trained for cooperative driving are plagued by \emph{mode collapse}: they converge to a single repeated action (selecting ``Maintain'' $99.7\%$ of the time in our environment), abandoning the lane-change and acceleration behaviors the task requires. Standard remedies---entropy regularization, exploration bonuses, and value decomposition---fail to resolve this degeneracy, as we demonstrate across an entropy-coefficient sweep and a multi-agent SAC baseline. We present CALM (Constrained Actor-Learning from deMonstrations), a hybrid imitation--reinforcement learning algorithm that prevents mode collapse through behavioral cloning (BC) pre-training on human driving data, followed by RL fine-tuning with a decaying KL-divergence constraint anchored to the BC prior. CALM is evaluated against six comparators---DAgger, GAIL, entropy-regularized MADDPG, QMIX-CALM, MASAC, and a rule-based controller---on a cooperative highway exit coordination task validated against the NGSIM naturalistic driving dataset. In a five-seed comparative study, DAgger achieves the highest NGSIM action agreement at $45.3\% \pm 0.9\%$ ($p < 0.001$ vs.\ all others), while CALM and QMIX-CALM eliminate mode collapse (action entropy $0.70$--$0.88$ vs.\ $0.06$ for the entropy-only baseline) and attain the highest RL reward among methods that preserve diversity ($1.35 \pm 0.05$). Anchoring MARL policies to human demonstrations via decaying KL regularization is an effective strategy for producing diverse, human-like cooperative driving behaviors.
\end{abstract}

Multi-agent reinforcement learning, mode collapse, imitation learning, cooperative driving, behavioral cloning, NGSIM validation
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Connected and autonomous vehicles (CAVs) promise safer and more efficient highway traffic through cooperative maneuvers, such as coordinated lane changes and exit ramp navigation~\cite{guanetti2018control,wang2021cooperative}.
Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) provides a natural framework for such problems, enabling decentralized policies that scale with the number of vehicles while being trained in a centralized manner~\cite{lowe2017multi,rashid2018qmix}.

Despite substantial progress, a critical failure mode persists: \emph{mode collapse}, in which all agents converge to a single repeated action (e.g., ``maintain speed''), regardless of the traffic state~\cite{haarnoja2018soft}.
In our cooperative highway-exit coordination environment, standard MADDPG training produces policies in which agents select ``Maintain'' $99.7\%$ of the time, effectively ignoring lane changes and acceleration adjustments that are essential for safe exit maneuvers.
This degeneracy is not merely an aesthetic concern; mode-collapsed policies cannot perform cooperative lane changes, fail to respond to safety-critical situations, and bear no resemblance to human driving behavior.

Existing approaches to address mode collapse include entropy regularization~\cite{haarnoja2018soft,ahmed2019understanding}, diversity-promoting objectives~\cite{eysenbach2019diversity}, and count-based exploration~\cite{tang2017exploration}.
However, these generic techniques are often insufficient in MARL settings with discrete action spaces and safety critical constraints. Entropy regularization alone may not prevent collapse when reward signals dominate, and exploration bonuses do not encode knowledge of what constitutes realistic driving behavior.

Imitation learning (IL) offers a complementary approach by grounding learned policies in human demonstrations~\cite{ross2011reduction,ho2016generative}.
Behavioral cloning (BC) can initialize policies with human-like action distributions; however, pure BC suffers from covariate shifts and cannot improve beyond the demonstrator~\cite{pomerleau1991efficient}.
DAgger~\cite{ross2011reduction} addresses covariate shift through interactive expert queries, and GAIL~\cite{ho2016generative} learns reward functions that encourage human-like behaviors.
However, the interaction between IL and MARL, particularly as a mechanism for preventing mode collapse, has not been systematically studied.

In this study, we present CALM (Constrained Actor-Learning from deMonstrations), a hybrid IL+RL algorithm designed to prevent mode collapse in MARL for cooperative driving. Our contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{CALM Algorithm:} We propose a two-phase approach combining BC pre-training on NGSIM human driving data with MADDPG fine-tuning under a decaying KL-divergence constraint. The constraint anchors the policy to the BC prior during early training and gradually relaxes, allowing for reward-driven improvement without mode collapse.

    \item \textbf{Systematic Empirical Study:} We conduct a comparative study (five seeds per method) of CALM against six comparators---DAgger, GAIL, entropy-regularized MADDPG, QMIX-CALM, MASAC, and a rule-based controller---on cooperative highway exit coordination, validated against the NGSIM naturalistic driving dataset with rigorous significance testing. We additionally analyze the properties of the decaying KL constraint, showing how it controls the divergence from human demonstrations during training.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work. Section~\ref{sec:formulation} formalizes the cooperative driving problem and defines mode collapse. Section~\ref{sec:method} presents the CALM algorithm and analyzes the KL constraints. Section~\ref{sec:setup} describes the experimental setup, and Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the results. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the findings. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Mode Collapse in Deep Reinforcement Learning}

Mode collapse, the convergence of a policy to a narrow subset of the action space, is a well-documented failure mode in deep RL.
Haarnoja et al.~\cite{haarnoja2018soft} introduced maximum entropy RL through the soft actor–critic (SAC) method by adding an entropy bonus $\alpha \mathcal{H}(\pi)$ to the objective to encourage exploration and prevent premature convergence.
Ahmed et al.~\cite{ahmed2019understanding} provided theoretical analysis showing that entropy regularization induces a softmax policy structure and analyzed its impact on optimization landscapes.
Eysenbach et al.~\cite{eysenbach2019diversity} proposed DIAYN for learning diverse skills without reward functions and demonstrated that explicit diversity objectives can maintain multimodal policies.

In the multi-agent setting, mode collapse is exacerbated by the non-stationarity of the environment: each agent's optimal policy depends on the policies of others, creating feedback loops that can amplify the convergence to degenerate equilibria~\cite{zhang2021multi}.
Standard entropy regularization may be insufficient in MARL because the joint entropy of the system can decrease, even when the individual agent entropies remain stable.

\subsection{Imitation Learning for Autonomous Driving}

Behavioral cloning (BC) directly regresses from states to expert actions and has been applied to autonomous driving since ALVINN~\cite{pomerleau1991efficient} and more recently in end-to-end approaches~\cite{bojarski2016end,codevilla2018end}.
BC provides strong initialization; however, it suffers from compounding errors owing to covariate shift, in which the agent encounters states during deployment that differ from the training distribution.

Dataset Aggregation (DAgger)~\cite{ross2011reduction} addresses this by iteratively collecting data from the learned policy while labeling it with expert actions, guaranteeing no-regret convergence.
Generative Adversarial Imitation Learning (GAIL)~\cite{ho2016generative} learns a reward function via a discriminator that distinguishes expert from agent trajectories, avoiding explicit reward engineering.
Kuefler et al.~\cite{kuefler2017imitating} and Bhattacharyya et al.~\cite{bhattacharyya2022modeling} applied GAIL specifically to driving behavior modeling on the NGSIM I-80 highway dataset.

Several studies have explored hybrid IL+RL approaches. Hester et al.~\cite{hester2018deep} combined DQN with demonstration data (DQfD), whereas Rajeswaran et al.~\cite{rajeswaran2018learning} used demonstration-augmented policy gradient methods for dexterous manipulation tasks.
Fujimoto et al.~\cite{fujimoto2019off} proposed BCQ for offline RL, constraining the policy to stay close to the behavior policy.
Nair et al.~\cite{nair2020awac} introduced AWAC to accelerate online RL with offline data.
However, none of these studies specifically addressed mode collapse prevention in cooperative multi-agent driving.

\subsection{MARL for Cooperative Driving}

MARL has been widely applied to cooperative driving tasks, including intersection management~\cite{chen2020cooperative}, traffic signal control~\cite{chu2019multi}, and highway merging~\cite{shou2020multi,letter2019efficient}.
The centralized training with decentralized execution (CTDE) paradigm~\cite{lowe2017multi,rashid2018qmix} is particularly suited to vehicular settings, in which vehicle-to-everything (V2X) communication enables information sharing during training; however, policies must be executed locally.

Zhou et al.~\cite{zhou2020multi} applied MARL to cooperative lane changing in mixed traffic, while Shou and Di~\cite{shou2020multi} addressed on-ramp merging coordination.
Yu et al.~\cite{yu2022surprising} demonstrated the effectiveness of MAPPO in cooperative-game scenarios.
Recent surveys~\cite{haydari2020deep,li2023reinforcement} have highlighted the growing interest in deep reinforcement learning for intelligent transportation.

Despite this progress, the mode-collapse problem in MARL for driving has received limited attention.
Most studies report aggregate performance metrics (reward and success rate) without analyzing action distribution diversity or validating human driving patterns.
Our work fills this gap by systematically studying mode collapse mitigation strategies and providing the necessary theoretical guarantees.

\subsection{Human Behavior Validation with NGSIM}

The Next Generation Simulation (NGSIM) dataset~\cite{ngsim2006,colyar2007us} provides detailed vehicle trajectory data from US highways and has become a standard benchmark for validating driving behavior models in the literature.
Thiemann et al.~\cite{thiemann2008estimating} used NGSIM to estimate acceleration and lane-changing dynamics, while Montanino and Punzo~\cite{montanino2015trajectory} performed trajectory reconstruction and validation.
Wu et al.~\cite{wu2023human} surveyed human-like autonomous driving approaches, emphasizing the importance of naturalistic behavior validation.

In our study, we used NGSIM data both as a training signal (via BC and DAgger) and as an evaluation benchmark, computing the action agreement rates between learned policies and observed human driving decisions.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:formulation}

\subsection{Dec-POMDP Framework}

We formulate cooperative highway exit coordination as a Decentralized Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (Dec-POMDP)~\cite{oliehoek2016concise}, defined by the tuple $\langle N, \cS, \{\cA_i\}, \{\cO_i\}, T, O, R, \gamma \rangle$ where:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $N$ is the set of $n$ CAV agents;
    \item $\cS$ is the global state space (positions, velocities, and lane assignments of all vehicles);
    \item $\cA_i$ is the discrete action space for agent $i$ with five actions: \emph{Lane\_Left}, \emph{Lane\_Right}, \emph{Accelerate}, \emph{Decelerate}, and \emph{Maintain};
    \item $\cO_i$ is the local observation space for agent $i$, containing ego-vehicle state and nearby vehicle information within a sensing radius;
    \item $T: \cS \times \cA \rightarrow \Delta(\cS)$ is the state transition function governed by the SUMO traffic simulator~\cite{lopez2018microscopic};
    \item $O: \cS \times N \rightarrow \cO_i$ is the observation function;
    \item $R: \cS \times \cA \rightarrow \R$ is the shared reward function;
    \item $\gamma \in [0,1)$ is the discount factor.
\end{itemize}

Each agent $i$ maintains the stochastic policy $\pi_i: \cO_i \rightarrow \Delta(\cA_i)$ parameterized by $\theta_i$.
The objective is to determine the joint policy $\bm{\pi} = (\pi_1, \ldots, \pi_n)$ that maximizes the expected discounted return.
\begin{equation}
    J(\bm{\pi}) = \E\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t R(s_t, \bm{a}_t) \mid \bm{\pi}\right].
\end{equation}

\subsection{Hybrid Reward Function}

The reward function balances local safety and global traffic efficiency as follows:
\begin{equation}
    R(s, \bm{a}) = \alpha \cdot R_{\text{local}}(s, \bm{a}) + \beta \cdot R_{\text{global}}(s)
    \label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha + \beta = 1$. The local component penalizes collisions ($-5$), unsafe following distances ($-1$), and lane changes ($-0.5$) while rewarding velocity maintenance. The global component rewards the average traffic flow and penalizes congestion above the density threshold.

\subsection{Mode Collapse: Formal Definition}

We define mode collapse in terms of the action distribution entropy as follows:

\begin{definition}[Mode Collapse]
\label{def:mode_collapse}
Policy $\pi_i$ exhibits mode collapse if its expected action entropy falls below a threshold.
\begin{equation}
    \E_{o \sim d^{\pi_i}} \left[ \mathcal{H}(\pi_i(\cdot | o)) \right] < \epsilon_{\text{mc}}
\end{equation}
where $d^{\pi_i}$ is the state visitation distribution under $\pi_i$, $\mathcal{H}(\pi_i(\cdot|o)) = -\sum_{a} \pi_i(a|o) \log \pi_i(a|o)$ is the Shannon entropy, and $\epsilon_{\text{mc}} > 0$ is a collapse threshold.
For $|\cA| = 5$ actions, we normalized the entropy to $[0,1]$ by dividing by $\log 5$ and set $\epsilon_{\text{mc}} = 0.1$.
\end{definition}

In our experiments, the unconstrained MADDPG baseline achieved a normalized entropy of $0.003$ (selecting ``Maintain'' $99.7\%$ of the time), thereby satisfying the collapse criterion.
The NGSIM human driving distribution has a normalized entropy $\approx 0.65$, serving as a reference for desirable action diversity.

\section{CALM: Proposed Method}
\label{sec:method}

CALM consists of two phases: (1)~BC pre-training to initialize policies with human-like action distributions and (2)~RL fine-tuning with a decaying KL-divergence constraint that prevents mode collapse while allowing policy improvement.

\subsection{Phase 1: Behavioral Cloning Pre-Training}

Given a dataset $\mathcal{D}_{\text{NGSIM}} = \{(o_j, a_j^*)\}_{j=1}^{M}$ of human driving observations and actions extracted from the NGSIM Interstate~80 dataset, we pre-train each actor network $\pi_{\theta_i}$ by minimizing the cross-entropy loss as follows:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{BC}}(\theta_i) = -\frac{1}{M}\sum_{j=1}^{M} \log \pi_{\theta_i}(a_j^* | o_j).
    \label{eq:bc_loss}
\end{equation}

The pre-trained policy $\pi_{\text{BC}}$ serves two purposes: (a)~it provides a warm-start initialization for RL training and (b)~it defines the reference distribution for the KL constraint in Phase~2.
After BC pre-training, we stored a frozen copy $\pi_{\text{BC}}$ of the policy parameters.

\subsection{Phase 2: Constrained RL Fine-Tuning}

Because our action space is discrete, we adapt MADDPG to output categorical action probabilities via a temperature-scaled softmax over logits: $\pi_{\theta_i}(a|o) = \text{softmax}(z_i(o)/\tau)$, where $z_i$ are the actor network outputs and $\tau$ is a temperature parameter. Actions are sampled from the resulting categorical distribution during training and selected using argmax at evaluation. During RL fine-tuning, we augmented the standard policy gradient objective with a KL-divergence penalty anchored to the BC prior:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{CALM}}(\theta_i) = -J_{\text{PG}}(\theta_i) + \lambda(t) \cdot D_{\KL}\!\left(\pi_{\theta_i} \,\|\, \pi_{\text{BC}}\right)
    \label{eq:calm_loss}
\end{equation}
where $J_{\text{PG}}(\theta_i)$ is the MADDPG policy gradient objective, and
\begin{equation}
    D_{\KL}\!\left(\pi_{\theta_i} \,\|\, \pi_{\text{BC}}\right) = \E_{o \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[\sum_{a \in \cA} \pi_{\theta_i}(a|o) \log \frac{\pi_{\theta_i}(a|o)}{\pi_{\text{BC}}(a|o)}\right].
\end{equation}

The coefficient $\lambda(t)$ decays exponentially over the training episodes:
\begin{equation}
    \lambda(t) = \lambda_0 \cdot \delta^t
    \label{eq:lambda_decay}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_0 = 2.0$ is the initial BC coefficient, and $\delta = 0.999$ is the decay rate.

The intuition is as follows: early in training, $\lambda(t)$ is large and strongly constrains the policy to remain close to the human-like BC prior and prevent its collapse. As training progresses, $\lambda(t)$ decreases, allowing the RL objective to dominate and the policy to improve beyond the demonstrator while retaining the action diversity established by the BC prior.

\subsection{CALM with QMIX (QMIX-CALM)}

We also instantiate CALM with the QMIX~\cite{rashid2018qmix} value decomposition architecture. In QMIX-CALM, each agent's individual Q-network produces per-action Q-values, which are combined through the QMIX monotonic mixing network to produce $Q_{\text{tot}}$. To apply the KL penalty, we derive implicit action probabilities from the Q-values via temperature-scaled softmax: $\pi_{\theta_i}(a|o) = \text{softmax}(Q_i(o, \cdot)/\tau)$. A frozen copy of the BC-pretrained Q-networks serves as the reference distribution. The combined loss is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{QMIX-CALM}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{QMIX}} + \lambda(t) \cdot \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} D_{\KL}\!\left(\pi_{\theta_i} \,\|\, \pi_{\text{BC},i}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{QMIX}}$ is the standard QMIX temporal-difference loss, and the KL penalty is averaged over agents. Gradients from the KL term flow through the individual Q-networks but not through frozen BC reference networks.

\subsection{Analysis of the KL Constraint}

We analyzed the key properties of the CALM objective to explain its effectiveness in preventing mode collapse.

\begin{theorem}[KL Divergence Bound]
\label{thm:kl_bound}
Consider the CALM objective~\eqref{eq:calm_loss} with the decay schedule~\eqref{eq:lambda_decay}. Let $\pi_t^*$ denote the policy that minimizes $\mathcal{L}_{\text{CALM}}$ in episode $t$. If the RL objective is bounded as $|J_{\text{PG}}(\theta)| \leq J_{\max}$ for all $\theta$, then
\begin{equation}
    D_{\KL}\!\left(\pi_t^* \,\|\, \pi_{\text{BC}}\right) \leq \frac{2J_{\max}}{\lambda(t)} = \frac{2J_{\max}}{\lambda_0 \cdot \delta^t}.
    \label{eq:kl_bound}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
At the optimum of $\mathcal{L}_{\text{CALM}}$, the gradient with respect to $\theta$ vanishes as follows:
\begin{equation}
    -\nabla_\theta J_{\text{PG}}(\theta) + \lambda(t) \nabla_\theta D_{\KL}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_{\text{BC}}) = 0.
\end{equation}
Since $\pi_t^*$ minimizes the composite loss, we have $\mathcal{L}_{\text{CALM}}(\pi_t^*) \leq \mathcal{L}_{\text{CALM}}(\pi_{\text{BC}})$.
Noting that $D_{\KL}(\pi_{\text{BC}} \| \pi_{\text{BC}}) = 0$, this yields:
\begin{equation}
    -J_{\text{PG}}(\pi_t^*) + \lambda(t) D_{\KL}(\pi_t^* \| \pi_{\text{BC}}) \leq -J_{\text{PG}}(\pi_{\text{BC}}).
\end{equation}
Rearranging and using $|J_{\text{PG}}(\theta)| \leq J_{\max}$:
\begin{equation}
    \lambda(t) D_{\KL}(\pi_t^* \| \pi_{\text{BC}}) \leq J_{\text{PG}}(\pi_t^*) - J_{\text{PG}}(\pi_{\text{BC}}) \leq 2J_{\max}.
\end{equation}
Dividing both sides by $\lambda(t) > 0$ yields the result.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The bound~\eqref{eq:kl_bound} tightens as $\lambda(t)$ increases and relaxes as it decays. With $\lambda_0 = 2.0$ and $\delta = 0.999$, after 300 episodes, $\lambda(300) \approx 1.48$, maintaining a meaningful constraint during training. Full relaxation ($\lambda \approx 0$) requires $\sim$7000 episodes, which is far beyond our training horizon.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Entropy Lower Bound Under CALM]
\label{prop:entropy_bound}
Let $\pi_{\text{BC}}$ have expected entropy $\E[\mathcal{H}(\pi_{\text{BC}}(\cdot|o))] \geq h_0 > 0$. Under the CALM constraint with $D_{\KL}(\pi_t^* \| \pi_{\text{BC}}) \leq B(t)$ as in Theorem~\ref{thm:kl_bound}, the entropy of $\pi_t^*$ satisfies:
\begin{equation}
    \E\left[\mathcal{H}(\pi_t^*(\cdot|o))\right] \geq h_0 - \sqrt{2 B(t)} \cdot \log|\cA|
    \label{eq:entropy_bound}
\end{equation}
where $|\cA| = 5$ is the action space size and $B(t) = 2J_{\max}/\lambda(t)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By Pinsker's inequality, the total variation distance satisfies
\begin{equation}
    \text{TV}(\pi_t^*(\cdot|o), \pi_{\text{BC}}(\cdot|o)) \leq \sqrt{\frac{1}{2}D_{\KL}(\pi_t^*(\cdot|o) \| \pi_{\text{BC}}(\cdot|o))}.
\end{equation}
The entropy is Lipschitz in the total variation for distributions over $|\cA|$ outcomes:
\begin{equation}
    |\mathcal{H}(\pi_t^*) - \mathcal{H}(\pi_{\text{BC}})| \leq \text{TV}(\pi_t^*, \pi_{\text{BC}}) \cdot 2\log|\cA|
\end{equation}
where we use the uniform continuity of entropy~\cite{cover2006elements}. Considering expectations and applying Jensen's inequality to the concave square root:
\begin{align}
    \E[\mathcal{H}(\pi_t^*)] &\geq \E[\mathcal{H}(\pi_{\text{BC}})] - 2\log|\cA| \cdot \E\left[\sqrt{\tfrac{1}{2}D_{\KL}}\right] \\
    &\geq h_0 - \sqrt{2 B(t)} \cdot \log|\cA|.
\end{align}
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
Empirically, we observed normalized entropy $\geq 0.80$ throughout CALM training, consistent with the KL constraint preventing collapse. The bound~\eqref{eq:entropy_bound} becomes tighter as $\lambda(t)$ increases relative to $J_{\max}$, providing a principled explanation for why the decaying schedule preserves action diversity during the critical early training phase.
\end{remark}

Note that, as $t \to \infty$, the decay schedule yields $\lambda(t) \to 0$, so the CALM objective in ~\eqref{eq:calm_loss} approaches the pure RL objective. This ensures that the BC constraint does not permanently restrict the policy from improving beyond the demonstrator's capabilities. Algorithm~\ref{alg:calm} summarizes the complete CALM training procedure, which comprises the BC pre-training pass over $\mathcal{D}_{\text{NGSIM}}$ and the subsequent constrained RL loop in which the KL penalty~\eqref{eq:lambda_decay} is re-evaluated each episode.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{CALM: Constrained Actor-Learning from deMonstrations}
\label{alg:calm}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE NGSIM dataset $\mathcal{D}_{\text{NGSIM}}$, initial BC coefficient $\lambda_0$, decay rate $\delta$, number of agents $n$
\STATE \textbf{Phase 1: BC Pre-Training}
\FOR{each agent $i = 1, \ldots, n$}
    \STATE Train $\pi_{\theta_i}$ on $\mathcal{D}_{\text{NGSIM}}$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:bc_loss}
\ENDFOR
\STATE Store frozen copy: $\pi_{\text{BC}} \leftarrow \pi_\theta$
\STATE
\STATE \textbf{Phase 2: Constrained RL Fine-Tuning}
\FOR{episode $t = 1, 2, \ldots, T$}
    \STATE $\lambda(t) \leftarrow \lambda_0 \cdot \delta^t$
    \FOR{each simulation step}
        \STATE Each agent $i$ selects $a_i \sim \pi_{\theta_i}(\cdot | o_i)$
        \STATE Execute joint action, observe $r$, $\bm{o}'$
        \STATE Store $(o_i, a_i, r, o_i')$ in replay buffer $\mathcal{B}$
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE Sample minibatch from $\mathcal{B}$
    \STATE Update critics via TD error (MADDPG)
    \FOR{each agent $i$}
        \STATE Compute $\nabla_{\theta_i} \mathcal{L}_{\text{CALM}}$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:calm_loss}
        \STATE Update $\theta_i$ with Adam optimizer
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE Soft-update target networks: $\theta' \leftarrow \tau\theta + (1-\tau)\theta'$
\ENDFOR
\RETURN Trained policies $\{\pi_{\theta_i}\}_{i=1}^n$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Simulation Environment}

We evaluated CALM on a cooperative highway-exit coordination task using the SUMO microscopic traffic simulator~\cite{lopez2018microscopic}. The environment consisted of a four-lane highway segment with three exit ramps (MultiExit scenario). Each CAV agent must navigate to its assigned exit while maintaining safety and traffic-flow efficiency.

\textbf{State space:} Each agent observes its position, velocity, lane index, distance to target exit, and the relative positions and velocities of neighboring vehicles within a 100m sensing radius.

\textbf{Action space:} Discrete with $|\cA| = 5$ actions: Lane\_Left, Lane\_Right, Accelerate, Decelerate, Maintain.

\textbf{Reward:} Hybrid reward per Eq.~\eqref{eq:reward} with $\alpha = 0.7$ (local safety) and $\beta = 0.3$ (global efficiency).

\subsection{NGSIM Dataset}

We used the NGSIM Interstate~80 dataset~\cite{ngsim2006} collected in Emeryville, California. While newer naturalistic datasets such as HighD and Waymo Open provide richer sensor modalities, NGSIM remains the standard benchmark for multi-vehicle cooperative highway studies because of its high-density traffic segments with synchronized trajectories for all vehicles in a congested bottleneck---precisely the setting our exit-coordination task targets. Following Thiemann et al.~\cite{thiemann2008estimating}, we extracted vehicle trajectories and discretized continuous actions into our five-action space using the following thresholds: lane changes are detected when the lateral lane index changes by more than $\pm 0.5$ lanes between consecutive time steps (mapping to \emph{Lane\_Left} or \emph{Lane\_Right}); longitudinal acceleration above $+0.5$~m/s$^2$ maps to \emph{Accelerate}, below $-0.5$~m/s$^2$ to \emph{Decelerate}, and the remainder to \emph{Maintain}. Lane changes take priority over acceleration. The resulting dataset contained approximately 50,000 state-action pairs used for BC pre-training and DAgger expert queries.

\subsection{Baselines}

We focused our baseline comparison on off-policy methods (MADDPG, QMIX, and the MASAC variant of SAC), because they naturally accommodate NGSIM demonstration data in their replay buffers, which enables the direct ablation of the behavioral-cloning constraint that is central to CALM. On-policy cooperative baselines, such as MAPPO~\cite{yu2022surprising}, are complementary but require a separate demonstration-injection mechanism (e.g., mixing expert rollouts into the on-policy batch) and are therefore deferred to future work.

We compared CALM with the following methods:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{DAgger}~\cite{ross2011reduction}: Dataset Aggregation with a $k$-NN classifier on NGSIM data as the interactive expert. The model was trained for 50 episodes.
    \item \textbf{GAIL}~\cite{ho2016generative}: Generative Adversarial Imitation Learning with NGSIM demonstrations. 300 episodes.
    \item \textbf{Entropy-only}: MADDPG with entropy regularization ($\alpha_{\text{ent}} = 0.01$) but no BC pre-training. 300 episodes.
    \item \textbf{QMIX-CALM}: CALM instantiated with QMIX~\cite{rashid2018qmix} instead of MADDPG. 100 episodes.
    \item \textbf{MASAC}: Multi-Agent Soft Actor-Critic~\cite{haarnoja2018soft,christodoulou2019soft} with automatic temperature tuning and twin Q-networks, adapted for discrete actions. 300 episodes.
    \item \textbf{Rule-based}: Hand-crafted cooperative exit policy (baseline).
    \item \textbf{BC-only}: Behavioral cloning without RL fine-tuning (upper bound on IL).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{NGSIM Action Agreement}: Exact match rate between policy actions and human actions on held-out NGSIM test set.
    \item \textbf{Similarity Score}: $1 - D_{\text{JS}}(\pi \| \pi_{\text{human}})$, where $D_{\text{JS}}$ is the Jensen-Shannon divergence.
    \item \textbf{Action Entropy}: Normalized Shannon entropy $\mathcal{H}(\pi) / \log|\cA|$ measuring action diversity.
    \item \textbf{Final Reward}: Average reward over the last 10 training episodes.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Training Protocol}

All experiments used five agents ($n=5$), discount factor $\gamma = 0.99$, soft update rate $\tau = 0.005$, actor learning rate $10^{-4}$, critic learning rate $2 \times 10^{-4}$, and replay buffer size $10^6$. Each method was trained with five random seeds ($\{42, 123, 456, 789, 1024\}$) for a total of 25 training runs. Statistical significance was assessed using Welch's $t$-test with Bonferroni correction.

\subsection{NGSIM Action Agreement}

The agreement rates should be interpreted in context: random action selection would yield $20\%$ agreement (1 in five actions), whereas perfect human mimicry is unattainable owing to the inherent stochasticity in human driving decisions. The rule-based baseline achieved only $25.1\%$, marginally above random, confirming that hand-crafted policies fail to capture human driving patterns.

This illustrates why action agreement alone is an insufficient metric and must be interpreted in conjunction with entropy and distributional similarity.

The entropy-only baseline achieves only $14.6\% \pm 23.8\%$ agreement, which is \emph{worse than random}, with an extremely high variance across seeds. \subsection{Mode Collapse Analysis}

The action entropy column in Table~\ref{tab:agreement} directly measures mode collapse prevention. Recall from Definition~\ref{def:mode_collapse} that an entropy below $0.1$ indicates mode collapse.

\textbf{CALM and BC-only achieve the highest entropy} ($0.88$), matching the diversity of the human NGSIM distribution. This confirms that BC pre-training successfully transfers human action diversity to the learned policy and that the decaying KL constraint in CALM preserves this diversity during RL fine-tuning.

\textbf{QMIX-CALM shows slightly lower entropy} ($0.70$) but remains well above the collapse threshold. The discrete nature of QMIX's $\epsilon$-greedy exploration may contribute to this difference compared with MADDPG's continuous policy gradient.

\textbf{DAgger achieves moderate entropy} ($0.63$), reflecting its supervised learning objective which does not explicitly encourage diversity. However, because DAgger is trained on aggregated datasets that include diverse human actions, it naturally maintains reasonable diversity.

\textbf{The entropy-only baseline catastrophically fails} across all tested coefficients $\alpha_\mathrm{ent} \in \{0.001, 0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.5, 1.0\}$ (30 runs total, 5 seeds each). Table~\ref{tab:entropy_tuning} shows that even the strongest entropy bonus ($\alpha_\mathrm{ent} = 1.0$) achieves only $0.24 \pm 0.17$ normalized entropy---far below CALM's $0.88$---with extreme variance across seeds ($\pm 13.9\%$ agreement). This comprehensive sweep rules out the possibility that our original entropy-only result ($\alpha = 0.01$) is an artifact of poor hyperparameter tuning.

\subsection{Task Reward Analysis}

Although human-likeness is our primary objective, task performance remains important for practical deployment. The reward column in Table~\ref{tab:agreement} reveals an inverse relationship between the NGSIM agreement and RL reward.

\textbf{CALM achieves the highest reward} ($1.35 \pm 0.05$) among methods that preserve action diversity, demonstrating that the BC constraint does not sacrifice task performance. The decaying $\lambda(t)$ schedule allows CALM to optimize the reward while retaining the action diversity established during BC pre-training.

\textbf{MASAC achieves moderate reward} ($1.08 \pm 0.18$) but with $3.6\times$ higher variance than CALM, reflecting less stable training. While SAC's automatic entropy tuning prevents mode collapse, the lack of demonstration anchoring leads to undirected exploration that is less sample-efficient than CALM's constrained approach.

\textbf{QMIX-CALM achieves good reward} ($1.01 \pm 0.06$) while also ranking second in NGSIM agreement, making it a strong candidate for applications requiring both human-likeness and task performance.

\textbf{DAgger achieves the lowest reward} ($0.44 \pm 0.02$) among trained methods, reflecting its pure imitation objective. DAgger optimizes action matching rather than the task reward, and human drivers in the NGSIM dataset did not optimize for our specific reward function.

This trade-off is fundamental: methods that closely match human behavior inherit human sub-optimality with respect to the engineered reward, whereas methods that optimize rewards may deviate from human-like behavior. CALM and QMIX-CALM achieve a favorable balance using human demonstrations as constraints rather than objectives.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Key Findings: Human-Anchored Diversity, Not Human Mimicry}

The central finding of this study is that \emph{human-anchored} diversity and \emph{human-mimicking} diversity are not the same, and the difference is measurable at the transportation level. DAgger attains the highest NGSIM action agreement ($45.3\%$) and an entropy ($0.63$) that matches the NGSIM empirical distribution ($\approx 0.65$) almost exactly; on these two policy-level metrics, it is the most ``human-like'' method in our study. Yet Table~\ref{tab:safety} tells a different story: DAgger produces $3150.5$ close-proximity events per episode, compared with only $42.5$ for CALM---a nearly two-order-of-magnitude gap. The mechanism is direct: NGSIM drivers in the Interstate~80 segment are cautious and slow (avg.\ speed $\sim\!11$\,m/s in a congested bottleneck), and blind mimicry inherits that caution along with the elevated exposure it implies. CALM's higher entropy ($0.88 > 0.65$) is therefore not ``over-exploration'' but task-conditioned redistribution of mass: the BC prior anchors the action marginals so that the policy never collapses, while the decaying KL schedule allows the reward to lift mass onto decisive lane changes and confident speed selection ($25.8$\,m/s avg., $33.3\%$ exit success). In short, CALM reproduces the \emph{structure} of human action diversity without importing the sub-optimality of a specific congested dataset.

Against that reframe, the rest of the comparison falls into place. QMIX-CALM sits between DAgger and CALM ($40.1\%$ agreement, $1.01$ reward, entropy $0.70$), and the monotonic mixer appears well suited to constrained IL+RL because it admits per-agent KL penalties without disturbing centralized credit assignment. The baselines that omit demonstration anchoring tell a complementary story: the entropy-only baseline collapses to near-zero entropy ($0.06$), despite an explicit diversity bonus and a six-value coefficient sweep (Table~\ref{tab:entropy_tuning}), confirming that generic entropy regularization is insufficient when the reward signal dominates. MASAC's automatic temperature tuning sustains high entropy ($0.93$), but fails on a different axis entirely: its NGSIM agreement ($24.8\% \pm 12.7\%$) has roughly $10\times$ CALM's seed-to-seed variance ($\pm 1.2\%$). The distinction between \emph{undirected diversity} (SAC) and \emph{structured diversity} (CALM) is therefore not rhetorical; it is visible in the variance of the learned policies. Preventing collapse is necessary but not sufficient; the diversity that remains must be anchored to human demonstrations to be both reliable and safe.

\subsection{Implications for Cooperative Driving}

These findings have several practical implications for deploying MARL-based cooperative driving systems. First, the correct safety argument is not that autonomous vehicles should mimic human actions, but that they should inherit the \emph{shape} of the human action distribution so that their behavior remains predictable to surrounding drivers while still being improvable under an engineered reward. The mode collapse observed in unconstrained MADDPG ($99.7\%$ ``Maintain'') would manifest in practice as vehicles that refuse to change lanes or adjust speed, thereby creating disruptions exactly at the exit ramps where coordination matters most; and the catastrophic failure of the entropy-only baseline and the high seed-to-seed variance of MASAC show that neither generic entropy nor learned temperatures are substitutes for a demonstration prior. Therefore, BC pre-training on a modest human dataset, such as NGSIM, is not an optional enhancement but a crucial inductive bias.

Second, the choice between DAgger and CALM is context-dependent, but the conventional framing of that trade-off is misleading: it is not ``human-likeness vs.\ task reward'' but ``human-mimicking vs.\ human-anchored.'' In mixed-autonomy traffic, where matching the action distribution of the surrounding human drivers is the explicit objective (e.g., a human-study evaluation), DAgger's $45.3\%$ agreement is the right operating point. In any deployment where safety and task completion matter---that is, in every real deployment---CALM's combination of preserved diversity, highest reward ($1.35$), and the lowest proximity-event rate in our study is the more defensible choice. QMIX-CALM offers a practical middle ground with strong agreement ($40.1\%$) and reward ($1.01$), and its value-decomposition architecture offers better scalability to larger agent populations.

\subsection{Relation to Prior Work}

This study extends our prior work on cooperative highway exit coordination~\cite{jeragh2026coex}, which introduced the Dec-POMDP formulation, hybrid reward function, and first comparison of MADDPG and QMIX for multi-exit scenarios. The previous study reported $54.83\%$ NGSIM action agreement for unconstrained MADDPG, which is consistent with the $54.8\%$ reported herein; however, it did not analyze action distributions in depth. The present work reveals that this seemingly high agreement is an artifact of mode collapse (Table~\ref{tab:agreement}), thus motivating the CALM algorithm as a principled solution to this problem. The two studies shared the same simulation environment and NGSIM validation pipeline, ensuring consistency across the results.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Mode collapse is a critical barrier to the deployment of MARL in cooperative autonomous driving. This study presented CALM, a hybrid imitation--reinforcement learning algorithm that prevents this degeneracy through behavioral cloning pre-training, followed by RL fine-tuning under a decaying KL-divergence constraint anchored to the BC prior, for which we derived KL and entropy bounds that formalize how the constraint preserves action diversity during training. Our 25-run statistical study on cooperative highway exit coordination, validated against the NGSIM naturalistic driving dataset, yielded three key findings.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{DAgger achieves the highest human-likeness} with $45.3\% \pm 0.9\%$ NGSIM action agreement ($p < 0.001$), making it the preferred choice when matching human behavior is paramount.

    \item \textbf{CALM achieves the highest task reward among methods that preserve action diversity} ($1.35 \pm 0.05$, with entropy $0.88$) and the best safety profile ($33.3\%$ exit success, lowest proximity events), demonstrating that the BC constraint prevents mode collapse while simultaneously improving both task performance and driving safety.

    \item \textbf{Entropy regularization alone is insufficient}: the entropy-only baseline collapsed to near-zero entropy ($0.06$) despite explicit diversity incentives, and MASAC's automatic temperature tuning sustained high entropy ($0.93$) only at the cost of wildly unstable agreement ($\pm 12.7\%$ vs.\ CALM's $\pm 1.2\%$), confirming that the resulting diversity must be structured by human demonstrations rather than merely enforced by an entropy bonus. Together these results establish that incorporating human behavioral priors through constrained learning is essential for MARL policies that are simultaneously diverse, human-like, and safe.
\end{enumerate}

Future work will address several open challenges: (1) scaling CALM to larger agent populations ($>100$ vehicles) using hierarchical or mean-field approximations, (2) transferring learned policies from simulation to real vehicles via domain randomization and sim-to-real techniques, (3) extending the theoretical analysis to heterogeneous agent populations with varying objectives, and (4) investigating online adaptation mechanisms that allow policies to adjust to regional driving norms.

The code and trained models are available at \url{https://github.com/mjeragh/VANET}.
