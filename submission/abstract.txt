Coordinating highway exits in dense traffic requires connected autonomous vehicles to execute precise, tightly-coupled lane-change and speed-adjustment maneuvers under shared safety constraints. Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) offers a scalable framework for this task, but MARL policies trained in cooperative driving environments are plagued by mode collapse: they converge to a single repeated action (e.g., selecting "Maintain" 99.7% of the time in our environment), abandoning exactly the lane-change and acceleration behaviors that exit coordination demands and producing vehicles whose actions human drivers cannot anticipate. Standard remedies---entropy regularization, exploration bonuses, and value-decomposition methods---fail to resolve this degeneracy in discrete-action cooperative driving, as we demonstrate empirically across an entropy-coefficient sweep and a multi-agent SAC baseline. We present CALM (Constrained Actor-Learning from deMonstrations), a hybrid imitation-reinforcement learning algorithm that prevents mode collapse through behavioral cloning (BC) pre-training on human driving data, followed by RL fine-tuning with a decaying KL-divergence constraint anchored to the BC prior. We derive KL and entropy bounds that formalize how the constraint preserves action diversity during training while allowing gradual reward-driven improvement. We evaluated CALM against five baselines---DAgger, GAIL, entropy-regularized MADDPG, QMIX-CALM, and Multi-Agent SAC---on a cooperative highway exit coordination task validated against the NGSIM naturalistic driving dataset. In a 25-run statistical study (5 methods x 5 seeds), DAgger achieves the highest NGSIM action agreement at 45.3% +/- 0.9% (p < 0.001 vs. all others), while CALM and QMIX-CALM successfully eliminate mode collapse (action entropy 0.70-0.88 vs. 0.06 for entropy-only baseline) and achieve the highest RL rewards (1.35 +/- 0.05). Our results demonstrate that anchoring MARL policies to human demonstrations via decaying KL regularization is an effective strategy for producing diverse, human-like cooperative driving behaviors.
